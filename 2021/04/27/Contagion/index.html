Contagion

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-04-27

Contagion, directed by Steven Soderbergh, is a movie that can only be described as hyper realistic after the events of 2020 and 2021. It explores a modern day pandemic of a deadly virus that has infected millions of people. The screenplay is written in an interesting way, as it follows a couple different stories at the same time. The first is about Mitch Emhoff who loses his wife and son to the virus after his wife travels out of the country on a work trip. There are also story lines from top medical professionals at the CDC and front line workers in the United States. Finally, there is also Dr. Leonora Orantes that travels to China to investigate how the virus came to be. On the other side of the story, there is the conspiracy theorist Alan Krumwiede trying to prove that the virus is blown out of proportional and that there are third party solutions available. It is interesting that this movie came out in 2011 because its commentary is so accurate to the current COVID-19 situation. Although not nearly as deadly, the coronavirus has had a similar affect on our lives and on the morality of society. Not only that, but just like in the movie, people have started to turn on each other, trying to find someone to blame for everything.

Watching this movie, while we are basically living it is a scary experience, and that is overall mood that is portrayed in the film. For someone watching this movie last decade, it may just seem like a odd spin on a classic dystopian film. So in a way, it has lived two lives; one as a dystopia and one as a docudrama. These two lives share some similarities rooted in the emotion of surprise or fear. The movie starts with the shockingly rapid death of Mitch’s wife and son, depicted clearly with up close shots of their dead eyes. Horrific scenes such as this one are used by the director to make the theme as impactful as possible and even show the gritty work done by medical professionals. There is another scene where Beth Emhoff’s scalp is removed, which definitely did not sit well with my fairly weak stomach. Contagion’s narrative is exponential, exploding rapidly to its conflict, and staying at the same level of suspense for most of the movie.

The next common emotion shared by both lives of the movie is sadness. There are many scenes that portray this emotion for various different age groups. The most impactful scene for me was the prom night for Mitch’s daughter. Prom and graduation are two of the most anticipated experiences for high school students around the world. The feeling that we will not be able to experience these things is quite sad and it made the movie that much more relatable.

Ultimately, the movie in today’s age is example of how much worse the situation could be. It scares you, but then makes you feel good that most of the more horrible events haven’t transpired in our world yet. Other than that there isn’t much to the movie other than its flashy features, so it does not stand out much from an artistic point of view. I would definitely recommend this movie to a friend because the sense of relief of how the situation was resolved brings hope that our current situation will be resolved as well.

Rating: 7/10

Works Cited:
Soderbergh, Steven. Contagion. Warner Bros., 2011.
