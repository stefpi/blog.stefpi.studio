Hotel Rwanda

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-04-05

Hotel Rwanda, directed by Terry George, is a movie inspired by Paul Rusesabagina and his heroic actions during the Rwandan Genocide of 1994. Paul was the hotel manager of the Hotel Mille Collines in Kigali, Rwanda and lived a stable life until the war between the Hutus and Tutsi rebels changed everything. His family, neighbours and even coworkers at the hotel were in danger, so Paul stepped up and did his best to protect as many people as he could. He opened the doors of the hotel as a safe place for anyone, no matter what religion, race or belief. He worked through countless setbacks and managed to feed, house and keep more than 1200 people safe. He risked his life and even stared death in the face when the Hutu army came to exterminate the temporary residents.

The movie was so real. It tried to portray the events in the rawest way possible, with no sugar coating. Knowing that what was shown had happened made the scenes feel exponentially more impactful. It hurt to see what one human can do to another without any remorse. I felt that the purpose of the film was to educate the audience on what happened and how Rwandan people had to struggle to survive because of the lack of foreign aid. I think that after watching Shake Hands with the Devil, I noticed how little Hotel Rwanda focused on the lack of attention the genocide was recieving. Hotel Rwanda did show how foreign nationals were flown out of the country, leaving behind the innocent civilians to die, and also how the UN was unable to help Paul because they were understaffed. I think that Shake Hands with the Devil was slightly more impactful because of its emphasis on how the rest of the world failed the Rwandan people. As a Candian citizen, it was more jarring to see how countries such as mine turned a blind eye to the situation hoping it would resolve itself. The lack of this topic in the movie is not a downfall because Hotel Rwanda is meant to be the perspective of Paul, who did not know about the diplomatic struggles or why the UN could not spare more soldiers to guard his compound.

The dialogue, props, setting, clothing and acting all played their parts in making this movie historically accurate and impactful. It was difficult not to be shocked by the horrifying scenes such as the riverside road carpeted with dead bodies. You may expect me to say that this movie is not for the faint hearted, but that is not true, no matter how weak of a stomach you have I think that this movie is a must watch. People around me have always taught me that if we do not know the past, we will repeat it. The film allows the viewer to explore the topic in a modern way, through screenplay. So no matter how much you know about the Rwandan Genocide, this movie is still a must watch.

Rating: 8/10

Works Cited:
George, Terry. Hotel Rwanda. United Artists, 2004.
