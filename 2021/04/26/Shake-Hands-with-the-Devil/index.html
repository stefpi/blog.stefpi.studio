Shake Hands with the Devil

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-04-26

The movie “Shake Hands with the Devil”, directed by Roger Spottiswoode is the story of Romeo Dallaire’s efforts to stop the 1994 genocide in Rwanda. It is a comment on how the world failed to help the people of Rwanda, while Romeo and his troops risked their lives. The movie set a mood that represented sadness, but also perseverance. No matter how many horrible things happened, the UN troops were there to try their best and never backed off. Even when ordered to retreat out of the war zone, Romeo refused. The deep empathy that he had for the innocent people of the region set the mood of the movie and also exposed him as a modern day hero.

Compared to Hotel Rwanda, this movie explores the more central events of the genocide. Romeo Dallaire often met with the leaders of each side, attempting to persuade them into a peace treaty. He even executed a plan to transport Tutsi refugees behind Tutsi lines and Hutu refugees behind Hutu lines, ultimately saving hundreds if not thousands of lives.

The movie creates a contrast between the place where the genocide is taking place and the killings themselves. This is evident when Romeo comments on the beauty of the country, with its luscious mountains and valleys. Rwanda is truly beautiful and pristine, mostly untouched by modern industrial developments. It is ironic that such a beautiful place was painted with one of the most gruesome and tragic events in human history. On top of this, the director chooses to focus on the story itself, choosing to leave out flashy action scenes or complicated camera techniques to be able to expose the most honest recollection of events.

Another significant element of the movie is the symbol of the handshake. A handshake is a form of agreement between two opposing parties. While Romeo hated the Hutu militia for what they were doing to Tutsi people, he had to shake their hand to form an agreement that the exchange of refugees would happen. Romeo had to sacrifice his morals and eventually his sanity because he was so close to the people responsible and he could not do anything but shake their hands.

Every part of this movie, from its honest story, to deep symbolism is what makes it so impactful. Just like Hotel Rwanda, it explores such a horrifying topic, but I believe it is more historically accurate and possibly less dramatized. I would definitely recommend this movie to other people, and I think that it is the perfect movie to watch after Hotel Rwanda. After learning about the perspective of Paul, a local, “Shake Hands with the Devil” offers another perspective, and how the limited help they could give was hindered by bureaucratic decisions made by cowards in the safety of their cushy offices. It is a movie that will break your heart, but just as I have said before, if we do not know history, we are bound to repeat it.

Rating: 9/10

Works Cited:
Spottiswoode, Roger. Shake Hands with the Devil. Canadian Broadcasting Corporation. 2007.
