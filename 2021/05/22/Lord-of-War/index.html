Lord of War

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-05-22

Lord of War is a movie directed by Andrew Niccol about an arms dealer named Yuri Orlov. Coming from a poor family in Queens, N.Y., he always dreamed of becoming rich and making a name for himself. His pursuit of his dreams often made him blind to all the chaos and pain he was causing. During his career he brought along his brother multiple times who seemed to be less smart and less interested in the business they were conducting. His brother, Vitaly, quickly became a more ethical than Yuri ever was and unfortunately developed multiple substance abuse problems during their trips. Part of the movie focuses of Yuri’s incompetence to help his brother escape addiction and in many scenes he is shown giving Vitaly cocaine before he is indicted into rehab.

The film focuses on a very important topic that has arisen after the end of the cold war. Gun stock piles around the world were left to rot, and business people took the opportunity to buy said arms at reduced costs and to sell them to modern day terrorists in third world countries. Yuri becomes one of the biggest arms dealers in the world, and it is evident that his trade causes millions of people to lose their lives at the other end of a gun barrel. Yuri’s defense mechanism to the atrocities that he commits is that he does not pull the trigger and that his products kills less people than alcohol or tobacco. The movie is an angry message about how gun trading turns people against each other and turns young children into killers. Not only that, but the ending signifies how bad the problem is. Even people like Yuri who are obviously guilty can get away with it because of the power they hold. The United States government hates them, but at the same time they use them to sell their own guns. To be honest, Yuri has some truth to his famous line; “if I wasn’t selling guns there would be someone who would take my spot in an instant”.

Although the movie explored an important subject, I believe that it does not have that much entertainment value, with subpar performances from the likes of Nicolas Cage. This is based on my biased opinion of Nicolas Cage and how he seems to play the same character in every movie. At times the film comes off as a little bit cringy, and after watching it did not stand out in any way other than the fundamental theme. To summarize, I feel that the movie had a great base idea about the problem of gun running, but the acting and supporting scenes did not seem to add much. The film did have a few scenes that stood out, such as when Yuri told Jack Lawrence that he will never go to jail, which were reasonably powerful. I would still recommend the film to a friend because it is an original take on a very prominent social issue. It should be watched, but less for the entertainment value and more for the moral value. Anyway, in the words of Yuri Orlov; I just write these reviews, I don’t control what you do with them.

Rating: 6/10

Works Cited:
Niccol, Andrew. Lord of War. Lions Gate Films, 2005.
