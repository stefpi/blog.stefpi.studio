Casablanca

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-05-25

Casablanca is one of the most well known pieces of American cinema, and has only increased in praise since its release in 1942. The plot revolves around Rick Blaine, the owner of a “gin joint” in the city of Casablanca, and his most peculiar dilemma. He must choose between his love for a woman or helping said woman and her husband escape German capture. A man and a woman who are in love, but sacrifice those emotions for a higher purpose (the defeat of the Nazis). In this sense it can be considered a sort of propaganda movie, but it does its best to hide it. In a way the plot tries and succeeds in glorifying patriotic sacrifices, which indirectly encourages more of these acts in America.

Unlike a lot of the other reviews out there, I think that the characters are not very relatable and for that reason it is quite difficult to get into the movie. I think this may be because of generational gap. Plus, the movie does not go very deep into any of the characters backstory other than for context on the relationship between Rick and Ilsa. Instead, the film relies on the juxtaposition between how close they used to be and how they can’t be together anymore to entice the viewer and further the plot. Before watching, I was warned that I may become bored watching it because of its very slow tempo (compared to today’s movies), but instead I found myself enjoying how deep and meaningful each scene was (probably because of the really good acting). Instead of constant action and meaningless dialogue, the film resembled a play because every conversation added to character development, plot and atmosphere. This brings me to talk about Rick’s character development. A pivotal part of plot was how much Rick changed during the course of the film. At the beginning he had a personal code: “I stick my neck out for nobody”, but by the end it is clear to see the impact he had on so many people. Most importantly, he stuck out his neck to help Ilsa, her husband and inevitably the allied forces, in an effort against the German armies.

In terms of camera work, there was a lot of emphasis on close up shots that were used to show the emotions of each character and they were most often used with Ilsa. They pictured her micro expressions that showed her confusion in certain moments. She was also mainly shot from her left side and with catch-lights to make her eyes sparkle and to make her face seem sad, tender or nostalgic. Other than that the film did not see any outstandingly creative shots, instead opting for more stable camera work that would let the acting shine.

Coming back to the idea of propaganda, Rick ends up showing his true “American” side when he pulls out his gun and starts bossing people around. He forces Renault to let Ilsa and Victor to get to the airport and shoots Major Strausser, just to make sure that the male lead fulfills his duty to Hollywood to kill at least one person. Past the sugar coating the movie is just war propaganda, but at least it doesn’t end in a classic cliché. At least they give up on their love for the greater good and sacrifice the “happily ever after” ending bullshit. Unfortunately, there really isn’t any closure at the end. What’s gonna happen to Rick after he kills the Major? I guess the answer is too gruesome to put in a movie that is meant to empower the population to be more patriotic. Or maybe they were just leaving room for a sequel… who knows?

Overall, Casablanca is a great classic movie that has held up its reputation even in the modern era. I enjoyed that the movie was mildly entertaining even for how old it was and if I was born earlier I would have definitely consider it as one of the best movies. I still think that it is a very historically important film in cinema, but to be completely honest it doesn’t rank that high compared to my favourite movies. I always have been interested in watching the classic films like Casablanca, so in terms of experiencing film history I enjoyed watching it. Ultimately, Casablanca was a great movie, with solid attributes, but it did not stand out.

Rating: 7/10

Works Cited:
Curtiz, Michael. Casablanca. Warner Bros., 1942.
