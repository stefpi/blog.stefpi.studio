V for Vendetta

Stefan Pitigoi
2021-02-09

A masked villain or hero? Depends on how you look at it. Maybe V is the hero of the people and the villain in the eyes of the government.

V for Vendetta is a grim movie about an unwanted future in the United Kingdom, where everything is controlled by the government. Even the music that the people are allowed to listen to is restricted. Anything that could harm the all mighty circle of powerful men is shut down, silenced and locked in a dark box. V, a masked hero is out to change this reality, and sets in a motion a plan to encourage the oppressed citizens to revolt. He instills the idea of the 5th of November, a famous day due to its relation to the creator of treason, Guy Fawkes.

Although presented in a body form, V is simply meant to be seen as the physical embodiment of an idea and that is why his face is never shown. The creator wants the viewer to not associate what he does with a face, because he does not represent one person. Just like it was stated at the beginning of a movie; “do not remember the person, remember the objective, for humans make mistakes or die, but ideas live on forever”. The screenplay personifies V by revealing his back story of how he was imprisoned, tortured and nearly killed by sanctioned experiments. This makes the audience feel attached to him and it motivates us to create justifications for his actions such as blowing up buildings that very well may have had people inside of them. V walks a fine line between terrorist and savior, but again that is completely based on which side you look at it from.

The setting of the movie is very important as it creates contrast between the environments developed by the government versus the ones developed by V and Gordon Deitrich. The city streets and buildings are dark, grey, old industrial buildings that seem void of life. Even the house of avid propagandist Lewis Prothero is made from black marble creating the feeling of high class, but also evilness. Meanwhile, V’s and Gordon’s illegal artifacts make their homes shine with a variety of colours, which was the director’s way of showing the beauty in life without the tyrant’s scrutiny. Although slightly stressful, the constant darkness that covers most of the scenes creates the perfect mood expected for a dystopian film. It gets the viewer in the right mood for revolution.

I believe that the movie was written for a young adult audience. This age group is developed enough to understand that the things are not just good or bad, but rather a mix of grey. The movie’s goal is to teach the targeted audience about government oppression and how even though they may seem powerless alone, they can make serious change together. My favourite quote from the movie that shows this theme comes from V when he said, “People should not be afraid of their governments, governments should be afraid of their people”.

In conclusion, V for Vendetta brings an interesting commentary on totalitarian governments and the power of the people. I would rate the movie a solid 7/10 because it is an important movie, but at times it includes unnecessary interactions. When I say this, I mean the relationship between V and Evey. Throughout the movie they grew closer together, but I found it odd that they would fall in love. Maybe it was a symbol for Evey falling in love with an idea, but I felt that the love was created to fulfill the Hollywood romance cliché and possibly create a larger target audience. I was satisfied in the end as V redeemed himself by prioritizing the lives of the many over his love of one girl, saving the plot and England in the process. Overall, I enjoyed it and I would definitely recommend this movie to anyone with 2 hours to kill.

Rating: 7/10

Works Cited:
McTeigue, James. V for Vendetta. Warner Bros., 2005.
